Blackbaud experiences Cyber Breach

The Vermont Food Bank has been dealing with the aftermath of a data breach at technology provider Blackbaud, which led to a ransomware attack. While donors have been supportive, the food bank's leaders have hired a law firm to investigate and protect its interests, along with those of donors and its 215 network partners.

Many organizations, including Planned Parenthood, the George W. Bush Presidential Center, Vermont Public Radio, the Rhode Island School of Design, and Human Rights Watch, have also received notices from Blackbaud. However, Blackbaud users have expressed dissatisfaction with the timing of the company's notification, which was several months after the attack occurred. Despite this, Blackbaud has reassured users that no credit card information, bank account information, or Social Security numbers were stolen. The attack was initially detected on May 14, and Blackbaud's cybersecurity team, together with independent forensics experts and law enforcement, prevented the cybercriminal from blocking their system access and fully encrypting files. Blackbaud confirmed that all traces of the cybercriminal and their attempt to regain access ceased by June 3, and they began working around the clock to prepare contact data and author customized, scenario-specific communications for each affected customer. Despite some customers questioning the speed of Blackbaud's response, they maintain that their top priority was to stop the cybercriminal and expel them from their system.

Blackbaud, a cloud software company, suffered a data breach caused by a sophisticated ransomware attack that compromised many nonprofit accounts globally. The company's cybersecurity team thwarted the attack before the criminal could lock down its network, but it still created concerns among the affected customers. While Blackbaud has apologized for the disruption caused, it has implemented additional measures to prevent future occurrences and is working closely with law enforcement agencies to mitigate the damage. Although the intruder's access was discovered, officials have declined to mention how many accounts were accessed during that period. The firm has confirmed that encrypted information, such as bank account details or passwords, was not accessible, and a formal report was made to the Information Commissioner’s Office in line with the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) obligations. Cybersecurity experts doubt whether Blackbaud can guarantee that the stolen data has been destroyed. Blackbaud hired outside experts to monitor the dark web and has denied any third-party involvement in the investigation.
